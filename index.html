A Decade of Change

The 1960s in America saw both  political and social change. 

In 1964, Congress authorized US military involvement in the Vietnam War, ostensibly to safeguard American interests and democracy.​​​​​​​ However, as the combat escalated and forces suffered heavy losses, the war quickly became unpopular.

"Marines drag casualty from street fighting for control of southern bridge, head across street to an ambulance in Hue, Vietnam, Feb. 4, 1968. Marines fight Viet Cong on both sides of the Perfume River within Hue City", 1968,  AP

"The United States regards as vital to its national interest and to world peace the maintenance of international peace and security in southeast Asia."
~ US Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (Declaration of War)

"That means sending our American boys into combat in a war in which we have no business, into which we have been misguidedly drawn, which is steadily being escalated."
~ Senator Ernest Gruening, 1964

"Tonkin Gulf Resolution (1964)", 1964,  ​​​​​​​Our Documents

Meanwhile, despite the successes of the civil rights movement, racial tensions and unrest remained over widespread inequality in America.

There was growing opposition to the incumbent Democratic administration's stagnant leadership and broken promises. Young college students were especially discontented because they felt underrepresented and powerless in the American government and society. ​​​​​​​

"Strikers on the ledge of Mathematics Hall, one of five buildings at Columbia University that students took over in April 1968", 1968,  ​​​​​​​The New York Times

"When the leading institutions and the top players act monstrously, the opposition is not morally free to stop thinking. It has to judge the consequences of its actions. The powers that be are not omnipotent. The powerless... have some powers."​​​​​​​
~ Todd Gitlin, Student Protester

"Marchers carrying Free Speech sign to the UC Regent’s meeting at University Hall, November 20, 1964", 1964, UC Berkeley Bancroft Library

On campuses throughout the nation, students organized rebellious demonstrations calling for reform in America. The movement centered on peace, equality, and defiance against establishment politics and culture. As thousands of ambitious youths communicated by protesting, it soon gained national attention.

"We were  for human rights and  against this...war. We...shouldn't have to be fighting this war, dying in Vietnam, if this country isn't recognizing our civil, democratic, human rights."
~ Bobby Seale, Protest Leader

" Demonstrate in the tens of thousands for stopping the bombing, immediate withdrawal of U.S. troops from Vietnam, liberation and self-determination for black people, an end to poverty and exploitation... the people, yes!"
~ National Mobilization Committee to End the War in Vietnam (Youth Organization)

As a result, young people became very politically involved. New youth organizations such as the Youth International Party ("Yippie") and the Students for a Democratic Society (SDS) encouraged people to openly rebel against perceived injustice. The student protests were unprecedentedly changing America.
